Motivation Scanner Candidates Relevance

Multiple sclerosis is now firmly tied to Epstein-Barr virus, and the lab works to find the antigen targets of the CD8 T cells that expand inside MS lesions. One leading explanation is molecular mimicry: immune cells raised against an EBV protein cross-react with a brain protein because the two share a short stretch of sequence. The clearest example is EBNA1, the EBV nuclear antigen, whose region near residues 386 to 405 is mimicked by the brain adhesion protein GlialCAM.

That made me want to ask a simple question from sequence alone. Where else along EBNA1 does the virus resemble a brain protein closely enough to matter? Below I scan EBNA1 against a panel of the CNS proteins most often implicated in MS, recover the mimics that are already validated as a check, and flag the strongest new candidates.

How the scan works

A T cell reads a short peptide, roughly eight or nine amino acids, held on an MHC molecule. So I slide a window of that size along EBNA1 and along each brain protein, and score every possible pairing with BLOSUM62, the standard substitution matrix that rates how interchangeable two amino acids are. High-scoring pairs are stretches where the virus and the brain protein look alike. The regions already shown to cross-react serve as positive controls: if the method is sound, they should rise to the top.

Analysis 2 — What the scan recovers, and what is new

Result
Validated mimics as controls, plus two strong new candidates

Using the panel at a 7-residue window, the scan cleanly recovers the mimics that are already published. The GlialCAM match sits right in the validated EBNA1 386 to 405 region. The alpha-B-crystallin match is the RRPFF motif at CRYAB residues 11 to 15, inside the validated 7 to 16 epitope. The myelin basic protein match is a proline-rich stretch next to its known cross-reactive region.

Two candidates stand out that are not the usual ones. EBNA1 near residue 575 matches MOG at ELLKDAI with six of seven residues identical, and EBNA1 near residue 500 matches MAG at DEGTWV with six of seven. Both MOG and MAG are well-known MS autoantigens, so a close linear match to EBNA1 is worth a direct test: synthesize the paired peptides and ask whether a single T cell clone responds to both.

Why this matters for the lab

The lab already has the hard part, the T cell receptors that are clonally expanded in patients. A sequence scan like this narrows the space of candidate antigens those receptors might see, turning an open-ended search into a short ranked list of EBV and brain peptide pairs to test in a binding or activation assay. The method extends directly to the lab's setting: restrict the windows to peptides that a patient's HLA can present, and rank by both similarity and predicted binding.

What I would bring

My background is in computational biology and sequence analysis. I am comfortable pulling and aligning protein sequences, working with substitution matrices and epitope prediction, and building small tools that make a screen easy to inspect and reproduce. I would be glad to connect this to the lab's TCR sequencing and antigen discovery work, and to help prioritize candidates for the bench.

Methods note

Sequences were downloaded from UniProt (accessions above). For a chosen window size, every window of EBNA1 is scored against every window of each brain protein using BLOSUM62, and the best-scoring pairs are reported with percent identity. Validated regions are drawn from published cross-reactivity studies (EBNA1 386 to 405 with GlialCAM; the RRPFF motif with alpha-B-crystallin 7 to 16; EBNA1 411 to 426 with myelin basic protein). Linear similarity is a screen, not proof. Real T cell cross-reactivity also depends on MHC presentation and on which residues the receptor contacts, so these are prioritized candidates for experimental testing, not confirmed targets.
